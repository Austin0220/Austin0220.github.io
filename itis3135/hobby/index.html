What is the Purpose of this Site?

This site is dedicated to building custom electronic clocks using Nixie Tubes.

What Are Nixie Tubes?

Nixie Tubes are glass tubes sealed with low pressure neon and argon gas that use high voltage electrical discharge, being the orange glow they are known for, to display information. Usually this information pertains to numbers, but depending on the tube, can display decimal points, mathematical symbols, or even letters.

What are Nixie Tube Clocks?

Nixie tube clocks are, as the name would suggest, clocks made from nixie tubes. They typically use hobby electronics and custom programs to function, and are typically very long lasting.

Why Clocks?

The reason people make clocks out of nixie tubes is mainly due to the visual asthetic of a nixie tube relative to other types of display technology. Sometimes you just can't beat that analogue glow!

Why more than one?

To be honest, the answer to this is the same as any other hobby, that being,"Why not, it's fun!" What keeps it interesting is largely that these clocks always look different, much the same as art. Compare these two clocks above for instance. They use the same components, Circuit board, metal, and angle iron stock. the only difference is the size of the tubes, and as a result of this, the proportions of the case.

How much time for each stage?

Each of the steps established in the Timeline page will have their own timeframe, listed here.

Planning

The shortest of the three steps, most of this will either be compiled for you if using a guide or easy to compile based on specs and design. What can take a while is if you need to do custom design worka as this requires some level of expertise and professional grade software. Overall if following a guide this should take a little under a month if not a week or two on and off.

Consolidation and Stepping

Gathering parts and waiting for shipping transit and supply chain availability will be the longest aspect of this step. In addition, the organization of parts will be something to consider, but shouldn't take more than a day overall accounting for shipping and out of stock parts, this should take around 3 months on average for most people. The hardest part to get is usually the nixie tubes, being no longer produced in many cases, they are only available on Ebay, and usually ship from eastern europe as most are of old Soveit make.

Assembly

Assemblly with be the longest part of the project unless you have the exact tools you need. Soldering, assembling, and testing the electronics will take up most of the assembly time, the rest will be prepping, building, and finalizing external components like the tube sockets, chassis, and mounting hardware to be able to take it apart and troubleshoot later down the road.

Order of events

This page will outline the basic requirements for building a nixie clock.

Planning

The planning phase consists of selecting your nixie tube type, either based on looks or cost. The two examples seen on this site are the IN-14 (the smaller one with a point on the top of the tube) or the IN-18 (the larger, smooth top tube) This will go on to determine the rest of your build, from there find or design a circuit board and determine the type of case you will want to build.

Consolidation and Stepping

Next is to order and consolidate your parts, in order of assembly. Traditionally you want to start with the electronics, get a working clock, then build the case around that. Divide each of these assembly steps accordingly so as to avoid damaging equipment or the clock.

Assembly

Requires Electronic Assembly and testing to ensure funcitonality, and then once everything is confirmed working, housing and other asthetic features, and ensuring solid and stable construction.
